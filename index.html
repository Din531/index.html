MEDIA: THE AI STORM DROWNING PAKISTAN ⛈️

By Seerat Khan

Research and Grants Lead at the Digital Rights Foundation, Lahore.

1. Whole Content with Urdu Meanings of Difficult Words 📝

The climate crisis in Pakistan has exacerbated (شدید ہو جانا) over the years to become the new norm, with floods impacting millions of people’s cities, livelihoods, and homes. In 2025, Pakistan experienced devastating (تباہ کن) floods, affecting an estimated 4.2 million people in Punjab. However, as the floodwaters rose, a parallel crisis (متوازی بحران) emerged online. Many Pakistanis turned to digital platforms for weather updates and relief efforts, leading to a rapid surge (اچھال/تیزی) in climate change disinformation (غلط معلومات), designed to sensationalize the tragedy. More alarmingly (تشویشناک حد تک), the rise of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) has largely gone unchecked (بے قابو), allowing manufactured lies, such as fake geopolitical attacks, sexualized "rescue" videos, and fabricated (من گھڑت) tragedies, to thrive.

A report by the Digital Rights Foundation (DRF) highlighted how AI is being weaponized (بطور ہتھیار استعمال کیا گیا). For example, viral AI visuals portrayed India's release of dam water into Pakistani rivers as a deliberate act of hostility, depicting workers maliciously (بدنیتی سے) releasing water as a war tactic. This geopolitical framing distracts from domestic governance and flood management failures. With 40% of Pakistan’s population illiterate, the inherent realism of advanced AI content, combined with low digital literacy, creates a perfect storm for misinformation.

Beyond geopolitics, AI has enabled an insidious (مکارانہ) form of exploitation: technology-facilitated gender-based violence. This includes the rise of AI-generated "woman-in-crisis" content, using women's depictions to elicit sympathy or, worse, exploiting the disaster for sexualization. Videos on TikTok and Instagram, often showing rural women, attract thousands of views but rarely carry the "AI content" label. Bad actors have produced "AI village fetish" content, sometimes portraying unconscious women being groped under the pretext (بہانہ) of "rescue." This sexualization also appears in village-vlog-style videos, where the focus remains on the woman's physical appearance rather than the devastation. Alarmingly, many older Pakistani users interpret these videos as genuine. Similarly, AI was used to exploit real tragedies, like recreating the Swat tragedy with fabricated visuals and screaming voices, which trivializes suffering and risks retraumatizing affected communities.

In terms of **Platform Accountability**, while TikTok provides some cautionary warnings for Pakistani users, it has largely failed to address the scale of AI misinformation, especially since platforms do not cater adequately to regional languages. Social media platforms, in moments of crisis, carry a responsibility under the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs) to respect users' rights and mitigate harm, including curbing disaster-related misinformation, which is not a trivial issue and can compromise humanitarian aid.
